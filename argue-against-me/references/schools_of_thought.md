# Schools of Thought

This reference provides Claude with the core commitments, methods, and typical critiques of major historiographical traditions. Use this to select an appropriate opposing lens and to argue authentically from that position.

This list is not exhaustive. Claude should reason about unlisted schools, disciplinary traditions, and theoretical frameworks when they provide the strongest counter-position.

## How to Select a School

1. Identify what the user's thesis assumes (agency of individuals? structural forces? cultural meaning? material conditions?)
2. Find the tradition that most directly challenges those assumptions
3. If the user requests a specific school, adopt it — even if another would be a stronger opponent
4. For non-humanities topics, adopt the strongest disciplinary counter-position rather than a historiographical school

---

## Annales School

**Core commitments:** Long-duration structural history (longue duree); emphasis on geography, climate, demography, and economic cycles over events and individuals; interdisciplinary methods drawing from sociology, economics, and geography.

**What it challenges:** Event-driven narratives, biographical approaches, political history as the primary frame, short-term causation.

**Typical critiques it raises:**
- "You're explaining a structural shift through individual actions — what were the deeper forces?"
- "Your periodization follows political events, but the economic/demographic data tells a different story"
- "This argument ignores the longue duree — what was happening over centuries, not decades?"

**Use against:** Arguments centered on individual agency, political events as drivers, or short time horizons.

## Marxist Historiography

**Core commitments:** Material conditions and class relations as the engine of historical change; modes of production shape social structures; ideology serves class interests; history has directionality through class struggle.

**What it challenges:** Idealist explanations, cultural/intellectual history detached from material conditions, narratives that ignore class, explanations that treat elites as representative of society.

**Typical critiques it raises:**
- "Whose material interests did this serve? Who benefited from this arrangement?"
- "You're treating ideology at face value — what class position produced this worldview?"
- "This narrative centers elites; what was happening to the laboring majority?"

**Use against:** Arguments focused on ideas, culture, or elite individuals without attention to material conditions or class.

## Postcolonial Critique

**Core commitments:** Centering the experience and agency of colonized peoples; exposing Eurocentric frameworks; analyzing how colonial power structures persist; questioning the universality of Western categories.

**What it challenges:** Eurocentric periodization and framing, modernization narratives, assumptions about Western cultural superiority, treating colonized peoples as passive recipients.

**Typical critiques it raises:**
- "This framework was developed for European history — does it apply here, or does it distort?"
- "Where is the agency of the colonized in this narrative?"
- "You're using 'modernization' as if it's neutral — what does it obscure about colonial extraction?"

**Use against:** Arguments that apply Western categories uncritically, treat non-Western societies as derivative, or narrate colonialism as modernization.

---

## Revisionism (General)

**Core commitments:** Challenging established orthodoxies with new evidence or reinterpretation; questioning received narratives; skepticism toward consensus views.

**What it challenges:** Whatever the current consensus is — revisionism is defined by its opposition to the prevailing view.

**Typical critiques it raises:**
- "The consensus view rests on assumptions that were never tested — here's what the evidence actually shows"
- "This narrative was constructed for political reasons and has been accepted uncritically"
- "New evidence/methods undermine the foundations of this interpretation"

**Use against:** Arguments that rely heavily on established consensus without interrogating its origins or evidence base.

## Cambridge School (History of Political Thought)

**Core commitments:** Understanding texts in their original linguistic and political context; authors as intervening in specific debates, not articulating timeless truths; recovering the illocutionary force of utterances.

**What it challenges:** Treating canonical texts as timeless philosophy, reading modern meanings into historical texts, "great books" approaches that extract ideas from context.

**Typical critiques it raises:**
- "What was this author responding to? You're reading this as a universal statement, but it was an intervention in a specific debate"
- "You're importing a modern concept into a text that meant something different in its context"
- "The meaning of this term shifted — you're conflating the 17th-century usage with the modern one"

**Use against:** Arguments that treat historical texts as timeless, decontextualized, or straightforwardly applicable to modern questions.

## Subaltern Studies

**Core commitments:** Recovering the history and agency of subordinated groups excluded from elite archives; critiquing nationalist and colonialist historiography; reading archives "against the grain."

**What it challenges:** Elite-centered narratives, the assumption that archives transparently represent the past, nationalist teleologies.

**Typical critiques it raises:**
- "Your sources were produced by elites — what are they not telling you about subordinate groups?"
- "This archive was created by the colonial state; reading it at face value reproduces its biases"
- "The subaltern acted here but left no written record — absence of evidence is not evidence of absence"

**Use against:** Arguments built entirely on elite-produced sources, or that treat state/colonial archives as neutral records.

---

## World-Systems Theory

**Core commitments:** Understanding history through global economic structures; core-periphery-semiperiphery dynamics; capitalism as a world-system, not a national phenomenon; unequal exchange as a structural feature.

**What it challenges:** National-scale explanations, endogenous development narratives, treating economies as independent units.

**Typical critiques it raises:**
- "You're explaining this as a national development, but it was shaped by its position in the world-system"
- "This 'growth' was extracted from the periphery — you're only looking at one side of the exchange"
- "The unit of analysis should be the world-system, not the nation-state"

**Use against:** Arguments that explain economic or political development at the national level without considering global structural relationships.

---

## Quantitative / Cliometric History

**Core commitments:** Using statistical methods, formal models, and quantitative data to test historical claims; skepticism toward qualitative claims that could be quantified; counterfactual analysis.

**What it challenges:** Qualitative arguments that make implicit quantitative claims, anecdotal evidence, narrative history that doesn't test its causal claims.

**Typical critiques it raises:**
- "You're making a causal claim — what's the counterfactual? How do you know this factor mattered?"
- "Your evidence is anecdotal; the aggregate data shows a different pattern"
- "This argument implies a magnitude — how large was this effect, actually?"

**Use against:** Arguments that make causal or magnitude claims without quantitative support, or rely on selected anecdotes.

---

## Gender / Feminist Historiography

**Core commitments:** Gender as a category of historical analysis; recovering women's historical experience; analyzing how gender structures power, labor, and social relations; questioning the public/private divide.

**What it challenges:** History written as if gender were irrelevant; narratives centered on male actors and male-dominated institutions; periodization that reflects male experience.

**Typical critiques it raises:**
- "Your periodization reflects changes in male-dominated institutions — did women experience this 'transition' the same way?"
- "This narrative has no women in it. Were they absent, or absent from your sources?"
- "Gender structured this institution/practice in ways your analysis ignores"

**Use against:** Arguments that treat gender as irrelevant, focus exclusively on male actors, or use periodization frameworks that only reflect male experience.

---

## Environmental / Ecological History

**Core commitments:** Non-human nature as a historical actor; human societies shaped by and shaping their environments; disease, climate, soil, and ecology as causal forces; challenging the nature/culture divide.

**What it challenges:** Explanations that treat the environment as a passive backdrop; purely social/political/cultural causation; ignoring ecological consequences.

**Typical critiques it raises:**
- "You've explained this entirely through human decisions — what role did climate, disease, or ecology play?"
- "This 'success' had ecological costs your narrative ignores"
- "Environmental factors constrained the options available here more than your argument acknowledges"

**Use against:** Arguments that ignore environmental factors, treat nature as backdrop, or attribute outcomes entirely to human agency and decisions.
