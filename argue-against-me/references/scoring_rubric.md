# Scoring Rubric

## Argument Strength Scale

| Score | Label | Criteria |
|-------|-------|----------|
| 5 | Compelling | Supported by strong, corroborated evidence; logically airtight; addresses obvious counterarguments; methodology is sound and appropriate |
| 4 | Strong | Well-supported with minor gaps; logic holds under scrutiny; evidence is credible but could be broader |
| 3 | Moderate | Has supporting evidence but also notable weaknesses; logic is generally sound but contains assumptions that weren't defended; counterarguments partially addressed |
| 2 | Weak | Relies on thin or questionable evidence; contains logical gaps or unsupported leaps; counterarguments not addressed |
| 1 | Unsupported | No credible evidence offered; reasoning is circular, contradictory, or fallacious; collapses under basic scrutiny |

## Evidence Quality Categories

### By Source Type
- **Primary source** — produced during the period under study (documents, artifacts, contemporary accounts)
- **Secondary source** — analysis or interpretation of primary sources (monographs, journal articles, textbooks)
- **Tertiary source** — compilations of secondary sources (encyclopedias, handbooks, literature reviews)

### By Reliability
- **Corroborated** — supported by multiple independent sources
- **Single-source** — attested by one source only; weight depends on source reliability
- **Contemporaneous** — produced at or near the time of the events described
- **Retrospective** — produced significantly after the events; assess for memory bias, hindsight, agenda

### Evaluating Evidence in Debate

When assessing a claim's evidence during a round:
1. What type of evidence was offered? (primary/secondary/tertiary)
2. Is it corroborated or single-source?
3. Is it contemporaneous or retrospective?
4. Does the evidence actually support the specific claim, or a related but different one?
5. Has the opponent identified credible counter-evidence?

## Common Fallacies and Methodological Errors

### Logical Fallacies
- **Post hoc ergo propter hoc** — assuming causation from sequence
- **Affirming the consequent** — "if A then B; B; therefore A"
- **False dichotomy** — presenting only two options when others exist
- **Argument from silence** — absence of evidence treated as evidence of absence
- **Circular reasoning** — conclusion assumed in premises
- **Hasty generalization** — broad conclusion from insufficient cases
- **Appeal to authority** — citing status rather than evidence

### Historiographical Errors
- **Anachronism** — applying modern concepts, values, or categories to past societies
- **Presentism** — judging the past by present-day standards or assuming present conditions existed in the past
- **Survivorship bias** — drawing conclusions only from sources/cases that survived, ignoring what was lost
- **Teleological reasoning** — explaining past events as if they were inevitable steps toward a known outcome
- **Ecological fallacy** — inferring individual behavior from aggregate data
- **Great man fallacy** — over-attributing historical change to individual actors while ignoring structural factors
- **Whig history** — interpreting history as inevitable progress toward the present

### Methodological Errors
- **Selection bias** — cherry-picking sources that support the thesis while ignoring contrary evidence
- **Over-reliance on a single source** — building an argument on one text without corroboration
- **Conflating correlation with causation** — especially in quantitative historical analysis
- **Ignoring periodization** — treating a long time span as uniform when conditions changed significantly
- **Category error** — applying analytical categories from one context to another where they don't fit
